Predictions come with uncertainty - learn to communicate it effectively

Marcus Finance Director

"Interesting analysis. But I need to know how confident you are in these numbers before I approve any budget changes. What's the margin of error here? I don't sign off on guesswork."

Every prediction from our regression model has uncertainty. This comes from two sources:

🎲 1. Natural Variation

Even with the same ad spend, traffic varies day-to-day due to factors we haven't measured (weather, news events, competitor actions, etc.)

📊 2. Estimation Uncertainty

We only have 100 days of data. With more data, we'd be more confident in our slope and intercept estimates.

Chapter 3

Correlation Isn't Causation

The most important lesson in data analysis

Victoria Brand Director

"Brilliant analysis! The data clearly shows ads drive traffic. I'm taking this to the board - we're doubling our Instagram budget!" But wait... can you really say the ads cause more traffic?

⚠️ Critical Warning

Just because two variables are correlated doesn't mean one causes the other!

There are several reasons correlation might not imply causation:

Confounding Variables

A third variable might cause both. Example: We spend more on ads during product launches, which also naturally drive more traffic.

Reverse Causation

Maybe we spend more on ads when we see traffic is high (to capitalize on interest), not the other way around!

Coincidence

With enough data, some relationships appear by chance. Ice cream sales correlate with drowning - both happen in summer!

Chapter 3

Spurious Correlations

Correlation: r = 0.95

This shows a strong positive correlation between "Number of Nicolas Cage films" and "Swimming pool drownings"

Does this mean...

Nicolas Cage movies cause drownings? Of course not! Both simply trended upward over time.

Real example from Tyler Vigen's "Spurious Correlations"

🌍 Real-World Case: Chocolate & Nobel Prizes

A study found that countries with higher chocolate consumption per capita have more Nobel Prize winners. Should Switzerland invest in chocolate to win more Nobels?

The truth: Wealthier countries can afford more chocolate AND better education systems. Wealth is the confounding variable!
🏥 Real-World Case: Hospitals & Death Rates

Data shows hospitals with more specialists have higher patient death rates. Should we fire the specialists?

The truth: The sickest patients are sent to hospitals with more specialists. Patient severity is the confounding variable - this is "selection bias."
A colleague says: "Our regression proves that Instagram ads drive traffic. The slope coefficient is significant!" How do you respond?

Chapter 3

Making Decisions Under Uncertainty

You Analyst

"Our analysis shows a strong correlation between ad spend and traffic. But correlation alone doesn't prove causation. We need to think carefully about what we actually know."

🛠 What Regression CAN Tell Us

  • Pattern identification: There's a consistent positive relationship in our data
  • Predictions with uncertainty: Given X, we expect Y within a range
  • Quantified relationships: The slope tells us the average association

❌ What Regression CANNOT Tell Us

  • Causation: We can't conclude that changing X will change Y
  • Direction: Maybe high traffic days prompt us to spend more?
  • Hidden factors: A third variable might drive both
Given all this uncertainty, why is regression still valuable for business decisions?
It isn't valuable - we should only use methods that prove causation
Regression helps us identify promising patterns, quantify uncertainty, and make informed predictions - even if we can't prove causation
Because a high R² guarantees our predictions will be accurate
It eliminates all risk from decision-making

💡 Practical Takeaway

Regression is a powerful tool for prediction and pattern identification. When interpreting coefficients, always ask: "Could something else explain this relationship?" Understanding and communicating uncertainty is essential for good decision-making.

Chapter 4

Staying In Bounds

Understanding when your model breaks down

Victoria Brand Director

"Your model says we get 195 extra visitors per £1k spent. So if we spend £1 million, we'll get 195,000 extra visitors! Let's go all in!"

🚨 Wait! That's Extrapolation!

Our data only covers £5k to £50k in daily spend. Predicting at £1,000k is far beyond what we've observed!

Linear regression has two critical limitations:

📎 1. Extrapolation Risk

The relationship we found applies to the range of our data. Outside that range, all bets are off!

📈 2. Linear Assumption

We assumed a straight line. But what if the real relationship curves? Maybe there are diminishing returns at high spend levels.

📋 Regression Cheat Sheet - Save This!

Linear Regression Equation
Predicts Y from X using a straight line
Y = β₀ + β₁ × X
Intercept (β₀)
Expected value of Y when X = 0 (baseline)
In our case: 5,200 visitors
Slope (β₁)
Change in Y for each 1-unit increase in X
In our case: +195 visitors per £1k
R² (R-Squared)
Proportion of variance in Y explained by X (0 to 1)
0.62 = 62% explained
Confidence Interval
Range where the true value likely falls (usually 95%)
Point estimate ± margin of error
⚠️ Correlation ≠ Causation
A relationship doesn't prove X causes Y. Consider: confounders, reverse causation, coincidence
⚠️ Extrapolation Risk
Predictions outside your data range are unreliable. Our safe range: £5k-£50k
⚠️ Linearity Assumption
Linear regression assumes a straight-line relationship. Check residuals for patterns!
