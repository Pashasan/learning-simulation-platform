In the random utility model Uij = Vij + εij, what does εij capture? ?Random Utility

Utility is decomposed into an observable part (V) and an unobservable part (ε). The researcher builds a model for V from data, while ε captures everything else.
The retail price of alternative j at the time of the purchase decision
Unobservable factors like individual taste variation and unmeasured attributes
The aggregate market share of brand j across all consumers in the panel

The Logit Model

From utility to choice probabilities

Professor Gupta Econometrician

"The random utility model is the conceptual framework. To get an actual formula for choice probabilities, we need a distributional assumption on ε. McFadden (1974) showed that one particular assumption gives an elegant closed-form solution."

The key question: What distribution should ε follow? Different assumptions give different models. The multinomial logit ?Multinomial Logit

A model where the probability of choosing option j equals exp(V_j) divided by the sum of exp(V_k) for all options. It assumes i.i.d. Type I Extreme Value errors. arises from one specific distributional choice.

Chapter 2

The Distributional Assumption

Professor Gupta Econometrician

"Different distributional assumptions on ε produce different discrete choice models. Normal errors give probit, which requires simulation. But one specific distribution yields a beautiful closed-form expression."

What distributional assumption on the error terms produces the logit model? ?Type I Extreme Value

Also called the Gumbel distribution. Its CDF is F(ε) = exp(-exp(-ε)). When errors are i.i.d. Gumbel, the difference follows a logistic distribution, giving the logit formula.
Normal (Gaussian) errors, which yield the multinomial probit model instead
Uniform errors bounded between zero and one for each alternative
Type I Extreme Value (Gumbel), giving the closed-form logit probability
Exponential errors with rate parameter estimated from the data
Chapter 2

Scale Invariance

Maya Research Assistant

"I tried the logit calculator and noticed something strange. When I added +1 to all three brands, the probabilities didn't change at all! Is that a bug in the tool?"

🔎 Maya's Observation

Adding a constant c to all utilities:

exp(Vj + c) / Σ exp(Vk + c) = exp(c) · exp(Vj) / exp(c) · Σ exp(Vk) = exp(Vj) / Σ exp(Vk)

The exp(c) terms cancel perfectly.

In the logit model, only utility differences matter because:
Consumers only evaluate products by comparing exactly two at a time
The Central Limit Theorem requires normalizing utilities before estimation
Absolute utility is unobservable — adding a constant to all leaves choices unchanged
The Gumbel distribution is symmetric around zero by construction

Estimation & Loyalty

Maximum likelihood and the G&L innovation

Dr. Rivera Research Lead

"Now that we have the logit probability formula, we need to estimate the parameters β from data. The standard approach is maximum likelihood estimation. But what made Guadagni & Little's 1983 paper truly influential was not just estimating the model — it was a specific variable they constructed."

Before we discuss their innovation, let's understand why we use log-likelihood ?Log-Likelihood

LL(β) = Σi Σj yij · log Pij(β), where yij=1 if consumer i chose j. Maximizing LL gives the same estimates as maximizing the likelihood, but is computationally superior. rather than the likelihood itself.

Chapter 3

Maximum Likelihood

Maya Research Assistant

"I understand we want to maximize the likelihood — find the β that makes the observed choices most probable. But why does everyone maximize the log-likelihood instead? Does that give different parameter estimates?"

📊 Likelihood vs Log-Likelihood

With 1,000 consumers and 3 brands, the likelihood is a product of 1,000 probabilities (all < 1). That product becomes astronomically small — too small for computers to represent without underflow.

We maximize the log-likelihood instead of the likelihood because:
The log transformation produces entirely different parameter estimates
It turns products into sums, is numerically stable, and is globally concave
Log-likelihood estimation requires far fewer observations than MLE
It is the only way to handle missing purchase data in scanner panels

Under IIA, the logit model predicts Blue Bus steals proportionally from all modes. But intuitively, Blue Bus should steal mainly from Red Bus (they're close substitutes!) while barely affecting Car or Walk. This is the fundamental limitation of the i.i.d. Gumbel error assumption.
Chapter 4

Independence of Irrelevant Alternatives

📚 IIA in the Coffee Market

In our scanner data, suppose Folgers and Maxwell House each have 30% share, and a store brand has 40%. If a new premium brand enters, IIA implies it draws proportionally from all existing brands — even though it likely competes most directly with the other national brands.

The IIA (Independence of Irrelevant Alternatives) property means: ?IIA

Formally: Pij/Pik = exp(Vij)/exp(Vik), which does not depend on any alternative other than j and k. This follows from the i.i.d. error assumption in logit.
Adding a new product to the market does not change any existing demand
The ratio of choice probabilities between two options is independent of other options
All brands in the choice set must have approximately equal market shares
Consumer preferences for each brand are independent across shopping trips
Chapter 4

Price Endogeneity

Professor Gupta Econometrician

"One last critical issue. When we estimate the price coefficient from observational scanner data, we often get estimates that seem too small — as if consumers barely respond to price. But we know from promotions that they do respond. What's going on?"

💰 The Endogeneity Problem

Consider two coffee brands: Brand A is premium quality (organic, fair-trade) priced at $8. Brand B is basic, priced at $4. Brand A sells well despite its high price because of high unobserved quality (ξ). In the data, we see high price and high demand together — making it look like price doesn't matter.

The true price effect is negative, but Cov(price, ξ) > 0 creates an upward bias, pushing the coefficient toward zero.

In scanner data, OLS price coefficients are biased toward zero because: ?Price Endogeneity

Firms set prices based on product quality, which includes features the researcher can't observe. This creates Cov(price, ξ) > 0, biasing the price coefficient toward zero (attenuation bias). Solutions: instrumental variables (e.g., BLP instruments).
Retail prices do not vary enough across stores to identify the price effect
Firms set higher prices for products with higher unobserved quality
Consumers systematically ignore price when choosing among familiar brands
The scanner equipment introduces rounding errors in the recorded price data
