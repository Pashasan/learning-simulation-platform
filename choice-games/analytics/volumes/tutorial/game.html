Chapter 1

Understanding Averages

Learn what the mean tells us about data

Imagine you surveyed 5 customers about how many times they visited your store this month:

2, 3, 5, 5, 10

The mean (average) is calculated by adding all values and dividing by the count:

Mean = (2 + 3 + 5 + 5 + 10) / 5 = 25 / 5 = 5 visits

Question 1

A coffee shop tracks daily sales (in cups): 40, 55, 60, 65, 80. What is the mean daily sales?

55 cups
60 cups
65 cups
80 cups

The median is the middle value when data is sorted. It's useful when you have outliers!

Consider salaries at a small company: $30k, $35k, $40k, $45k, $200k

Mean = $70k (pulled up by the $200k outlier)
Median = $40k (the actual middle value)

The median better represents a "typical" salary here!

Question 2

House prices in a neighborhood (in $000s): 200, 220, 240, 250, 900. Which measure better represents a "typical" house price?

Mean ($362k) - it uses all the data
Median ($240k) - the outlier skews the mean
Maximum ($900k) - shows what's possible
They're equally good measures
Chapter 2

Understanding Spread

Learn why variability matters

Two basketball players both average 20 points per game:

Player A: 18, 19, 20, 21, 22 (consistent)

Player B: 5, 10, 20, 30, 35 (variable)

The range (max - min) shows spread:

Player A: Range = 22 - 18 = 4 points
Player B: Range = 35 - 5 = 30 points

Same average, very different consistency!

Question 3

You're choosing a supplier. Both deliver in 5 days on average, but:

Supplier A: Always delivers in 4-6 days
Supplier B: Sometimes 1 day, sometimes 10 days

Which is more reliable for planning?

Supplier A - low variability means predictable
Supplier B - sometimes faster is better
They're equally reliable - same average
Can't tell without more data

Key Takeaways

Mean (Average)
Sum of all values divided by count. Sensitive to outliers.
Median
Middle value when sorted. Better for skewed data.
Range
Maximum minus minimum. Shows total spread.
Variability
How spread out data is. Low = consistent, High = variable.
