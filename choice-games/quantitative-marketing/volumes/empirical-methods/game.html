Chapter 1

Model Primitives

The building blocks of every empirical model

Professor Chen Faculty Advisor

"Every economic model is built from a small set of fundamental components -- what Reiss calls primitives ? Primitives

The foundational building blocks of an economic model. They describe what agents want, what they can do, what they know, and how they interact. Critically, they are "deep" -- they don't change when policies change. . These are the 'deep parameters' that don't change when policies change. Let me show you what they are."

Chapter 1

The Four Primitives

Click each box to reveal the building blocks of every model

♥ Preferences ▶
Utility functions -- what agents want. Consumers maximize utility over products; firms maximize profit. These encode tastes, risk attitudes, and objectives.
⚙ Technology ▶
Production functions and cost structures -- what agents can do. This covers manufacturing capabilities, distribution networks, and resource constraints that shape feasible actions.
👁 Information Structure ▶
What agents observe and know -- who sees what, and when. Do firms observe rival costs? Do consumers know product quality? Asymmetric information drives many marketing phenomena.
⚖ Equilibrium Concept ▶
How agents interact -- the "rules of the game." Nash equilibrium, Stackelberg leadership, competitive markets -- this determines how individual decisions aggregate into market outcomes.

"Good, you've explored the primitives. Now let me test whether you can distinguish them from outcomes ? Outcomes vs. Primitives

Outcomes (like market share, prices, or quantities sold) are what we observe in data. Primitives are the deeper forces that generate those outcomes. The whole point of structural modeling is to go from observed outcomes back to primitives. . One of these is not like the others."

Which of the following is NOT one of Reiss's four model primitives?

Market Share -- the fraction of demand captured by each firm
Technology -- the production functions and cost structures agents face

Chapter 1

Deep Parameters

Professor Chen Faculty Advisor

"Reiss calls these 'deep parameters.' That word 'deep' is doing real work -- it tells you something crucial about why these primitives are so valuable for policy analysis ? Policy Invariance (Lucas Critique)

If a parameter changes whenever policy changes, you can't use it to predict outcomes under new policies. Deep parameters, by definition, are stable across policy regimes -- making them the only reliable foundation for counterfactual analysis. ."

Why are model primitives considered "deep parameters"?
They require advanced computational methods and large datasets to estimate
They remain stable even when market policies or conditions change
They capture surface-level patterns that are easy to observe in data
They can only be recovered using structural econometric models

Chapter 2

The Inverse Problem

Dr. Patel Senior PhD Student

"The forward direction is easy conceptually. But as empiricists, we don't get to choose the primitives and watch outcomes unfold. We start with the data. So what's the real challenge ? The Inverse Problem

In the forward direction, primitives generate data through optimization and equilibrium. The inverse problem asks: given only the observed data, can we recover the primitives that generated it? This is fundamentally harder because many different sets of primitives might produce similar-looking data. ?"

The fundamental empirical challenge is:
Collecting large enough datasets to achieve statistical power
Choosing the right econometric software for the analysis
Recovering the primitives that generated the data we observe
Running computations fast enough to meet publication deadlines

Descriptive, reduced-form, and structural approaches

Chapter 3

Method Comparison

Click each method to see what it recovers and what it cannot do

Descriptive Methods

Recovers: Patterns, correlations, and distributions in the data -- no primitives
Example Q: "What is the average price sensitivity across customer segments?"
Cannot do: Cannot establish causation or predict outcomes under new policies

Reduced-Form Methods

Recovers: Causal effects of specific interventions (average treatment effects)
Example Q: "Did this price promotion cause a 30% increase in sales?"
Cannot do: Cannot extrapolate to untested policies or optimize across all alternatives

Structural Methods

Recovers: Deep primitives (preferences, costs) enabling full counterfactual analysis
Example Q: "What is the profit-maximizing price if a new competitor enters?"
Cannot do: Cannot avoid strong modeling assumptions -- results depend on model being correct
Alex Junior PhD Student

"Oh, I see -- so structural methods are more powerful but require stronger assumptions. Each method answers a different type of question. Got it... I think."

Chapter 4

Identification & Model-Free Evidence

When can we learn what we need from data?

Professor Chen Faculty Advisor

"Final chapter. Before you can estimate anything, you need to ask a more fundamental question: can this parameter even be learned from data ? Identification

A parameter is identified if different values of it produce different distributions of observable data. This is a property of the model, not the data. Even with infinite data, an unidentified parameter cannot be recovered. ? That's identification. And sometimes, the simplest approach -- an experiment -- gives you all you need. But not always."

Chapter 4

What Is Identification?

Dr. Patel Senior PhD Student

"You just saw the demo. Now, Alex said something earlier that worried me -- they said a parameter is 'identified' when it passes a significance test. That's dangerously wrong. Can you give the correct definition?"

A parameter is "identified" if:
It has been published in a top-tier peer-reviewed economics journal
The sample contains at least 10,000 observations for reliable estimation
Different values of it produce distinguishable distributions of observables
It passes a standard statistical significance test at the 5% level
Chapter 4

Model-Free Evidence

Professor Chen Faculty Advisor

"Last question. Experiments are the gold standard for causal inference, and sometimes their results are all you need ? Model-Free Evidence

Evidence from randomized experiments that doesn't require a structural model to interpret. Sufficient when the decision matches the experiment exactly and you only need average effects. Insufficient for out-of-sample predictions, welfare analysis, or optimizing over untested alternatives. . But when exactly? This is a question that separates careful researchers from careless ones."

When is model-free (experimental) evidence sufficient for a marketing decision?
Always -- well-designed experiments are strictly superior to any model
When the planned policy exactly matches the experiment and only average effects matter
Never -- structural models are necessary for every substantive business decision
Only when the firm operates exclusively in digital marketing channels
