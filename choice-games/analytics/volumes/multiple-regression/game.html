Chapter 3

Which Channels Really Matter?

P-values and statistical significance

Alex CEO

"Nice model, Maya. But how do we know these effects are real and not just noise in the data? I need statistical proof before I approve this budget allocation."

Every coefficient has a p-value that tells us the probability of seeing that result by chance if the channel had no real effect.

✅ p < 0.05 (Significant)

  • Less than 5% chance of fluke
  • The effect is probably real
  • Safe to include in budget decisions

⚠️ p ≥ 0.05 (Not Significant)

  • Could be random noise
  • Can't confidently claim an effect
  • Need more data or reconsider

Chapter 4

The Collinearity Problem

When channels are correlated

Jordan Data Analyst

"Maya, I found a problem! Google and Facebook spend are highly correlated - when you increase one, you usually increase the other. This makes it hard to separate their individual effects."

Multicollinearity occurs when predictor variables are correlated with each other. This inflates standard errors and makes individual coefficients unreliable.

🤔 The Problem

  • Hard to isolate each channel's effect
  • Coefficients become unstable
  • Wide confidence intervals

📊 VIF (Variance Inflation Factor)

  • VIF = 1: No correlation
  • VIF > 5: Moderate concern
  • VIF > 10: Serious problem
Chapter 4

Building VIF Intuition

What VIF really measures and why it matters

🎯 The Core Idea

VIF answers: "How much harder is it to estimate this channel's effect because it moves together with other channels?"

🎮 Try It!

Adjust correlation between Google & Facebook spend:

Independent Highly Correlated
Correlation (r) 0.00
VIF ? VIF = 1 / (1 - R²)

R² is from regressing this variable on all others. If perfectly predicted by others (R²=1), VIF→∞. If independent (R²=0), VIF=1.

Why "Variance Inflation"? VIF shows how much the standard error is inflated. VIF=5 means SE is √5 ≈ 2.2x larger than with no collinearity. 1.00
Confidence Interval Width ±0.8

✓ Variables are independent - coefficient estimates are precise!

💡 What You Just Saw

As correlation increases, the scatter plot becomes more "line-like" - the two channels move together. This makes it impossible to tell which one is driving results.

⚠️ High VIF doesn't mean your model is wrong - it means you're less certain about individual channel effects. The combined prediction can still be excellent!

📋 Multiple Regression Cheat Sheet - Save This!

Multiple Regression Equation
Predicts Y from multiple X variables simultaneously
Y = β₀ + β₁X₁ + β₂X₂ + ... + ε
Coefficient Interpretation
Effect of Xⱼ on Y, holding all other variables constant
"Each $1K on LinkedIn → +4.2 conversions"
P-value
Probability of seeing this result if there's no real effect
p < 0.05 = statistically significant
Confidence Interval
Range where the true coefficient likely falls (95% confidence)
CI includes 0 → effect might not exist
VIF (Variance Inflation Factor)
Measures how much collinearity inflates coefficient uncertainty
VIF = 1/(1-R²) | VIF > 5 = concern
R² (R-Squared)
Proportion of variance in Y explained by all X variables together
0.82 = 82% of variation explained
⚠️ Multicollinearity
When predictors are correlated with each other. Makes individual effects hard to estimate, but predictions can still be good.
⚠️ Dummy Variables
Categorical variables (0/1) like "Launch Month". Coefficient = difference from reference category, holding other vars constant.
